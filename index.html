ONLINE-RETAIL · 12-MONTH SEGMENTATION & RETENTION READ · DEC 2010 – NOV 2011

Two segments drive 71% of revenue — and newer cohorts are slipping away faster than they used to.

This report reads the UCI Online Retail dataset — ~541,000 transactions from a UK gifts-and-homewares retailer over 12 months — and asks five blunt questions about who is paying the bills, who is leaving, and where the next pound of marketing spend should go.

The headline read is healthy: £9.32M of revenue across 4,330 customers, with two-thirds of customers placing more than one order. The structural read is more interesting: revenue is heavily concentrated in two top segments, and the cohorts acquired after the first three months of 2011 retain at less than half the rate of the original cohort.

The central finding. Champions and Loyal Customers — 1,282 customers, 30% of the base — account for 71% of revenue. Meanwhile, month-1 retention has slipped from 36.6% in the December 2010 cohort to 15.0% by March 2011, and never recovers. The highest-leverage move is targeted retention spend on the 341 customers in At Risk, Cannot Lose Them, and About to Sleep — a small pool, but £574k of revenue at stake.

01  ·  Revenue & Reach

What is total revenue, AOV, and repeat purchase rate?

Sizing the box: how big is the business, what does an order look like, and how many customers come back?

The first read on the business is unusually strong on stickiness. The retailer turned over £9.32M across 12 months on 4,330 distinct customers spread across 38 countries — meaningful scale for a single-channel gifts-and-homewares operator.

Order shape and repeat behaviour both signal a B2B-leaning customer base: an AOV of £447 per invoice (well above a typical consumer e-commerce basket) and a 64.4% repeat-purchase rate. Together those numbers say "the customers we already have matter more than new acquisition" — and the next section makes that case quantitatively.

Headline KPIs  |  Total revenue, active customers, AOV, and repeat-purchase rate, Dec 2010 – Nov 2011.
  • Size £9.32M turnover across 4,330 distinct customers in 38 countries — meaningful scale for a single-channel gifts retailer.
  • Order shape AOV of £447 per invoice is well above a typical consumer basket — consistent with a B2B-leaning order book.
  • Repeat behaviour 64.4% of customers (2,756 of 4,330) placed two or more orders — unusually strong stickiness, and the reason segment concentration is the next question.

Two-thirds of customers come back. The next question is whether those repeat customers are evenly distributed — or whether a small group is doing most of the work.

Next: which customer segments actually contribute the revenue.
02  ·  Segment Concentration

Which customer segments contribute the most revenue?

Splitting customers into 11 named segments by their recency, frequency, and spend — then asking how revenue is distributed.

Stickiness is fine if the revenue is broadly distributed; concentration is fine if the customer base is healthy. With 11 named segments derived from quintile-binned recency, frequency, and spend scores (the standard Putler grid), the picture is sharply top-heavy.

Champions — just 511 customers, about 12% of the base — contribute 44.9% of revenue. Add Loyal Customers (771 customers) and the top two segments together carry 71% of all revenue from 30% of the base. That is a textbook Pareto and the structural fact that drives every recommendation in this report.

Next: what behavioural features actually predict who becomes a high-value customer in the first place.
03  ·  Drivers of High Value

Which factors predict high lifetime value?

Going beyond "Champions are valuable" — using a model to ask what actually moves a customer into the top tier.

Knowing that Champions are valuable does not tell us how to make more of them. To get at that, we trained a model to predict whether a customer falls in the top quintile of net spend from their behavioural features alone — recency, frequency, tenure, average basket size, average unit price, distinct product count, geography, and cancellation rate.

Frequency is unsurprisingly the headline driver. The interesting story is what comes next: average basket size is a near-equal second — meaningfully more important than average unit price. The implication for marketing is the cleaner of two routes: bundles and free-shipping thresholds (which grow basket size) beat premium-SKU pushes (which grow unit price) for converting customers into the top tier.

Feature importance — what predicts top-quintile spend  |  Coral = behavioural / customer features (the actionable signal). Navy = recency & frequency.
  • Frequency Purchase frequency is the strongest driver (importance ≈ 0.219). Expected, but useful: frequency can be moved with lifecycle programmes.
  • Basket size beats unit price Average basket size is a near-equal second (0.201); average unit price is a distant third (0.042). Push bundles and free-shipping thresholds, not premium SKUs.
  • Clean separation The model flags top-quintile customers with 95.6% precision and 95.3% recall — we can identify likely-high-value customers from behaviour alone, well before a full year of spend accumulates.

Beyond "they buy more often," the strongest behavioural lever is basket size, not price point. Bundles, multi-pack offers, and free-shipping thresholds are the cleaner play than pushing premium SKUs.

Next: a check on whether the customer base is being topped up at the same quality.
04  ·  Cohort Health

Are newer customers staying with us as well as older ones?

Concentration is fine if the funnel keeps refilling at the same quality — and dangerous if it doesn't. Cohort retention is the test.

Grouping every customer by the month of their first purchase, then tracking what share of each cohort is still buying in each subsequent month, gives us a clean read on whether acquisition is delivering similar-quality customers across the year. Unobserved cells (e.g. month +11 for the November 2011 cohort) are masked in grey to avoid the false-trend pitfall.

The picture is uncomfortable. The first cohort (December 2010) retains 36.6% of customers at month +1; the worst cohort (March 2011) drops to 15.0%, less than half. No later cohort recovers the December baseline. Topline revenue grew across the year, so the business looks healthy on the headline — but the cohort view shows a chunk of that growth is coming from less sticky customers, which is a warning rather than a victory.

  • First cohort is best The December 2010 cohort retains 36.6% of customers at month +1 — the highest of any cohort in the window.
  • Newer cohorts retain worse The March 2011 cohort drops to 15.0% at month +1, less than half the December figure. Across all 12 cohorts, no later cohort recovers the baseline.
  • Acquisition quality is degrading Topline revenue rose across the year, but cohort retention shows it is being delivered by less sticky customers — a warning that aggregate growth hides.

The funnel is filling, but with worse-quality customers. Acquisition quality, not just acquisition volume, deserves to be a tracked KPI — investigate the channel mix change between early and mid 2011.

Next: where the highest-leverage retention spend goes today.
05  ·  Retention Targets

Which 3 segments should we target for retention campaigns?

Pulling the segmentation and cohort views together: where is the highest-leverage retention spend?

The retention pool is small but valuable. Three segments — At Risk (high-value customers who have gone quiet), Cannot Lose Them (highest-value customers slipping away), and About to Sleep (early-warning tier) — together hold 341 customers (8% of the base) and £574k of revenue (7.2% of the total). That is manageable for a focused campaign, not a mass-marketing exercise.

The three pools deserve different intensity. Cannot Lose Them is small (38 customers) but extraordinarily concentrated — an average of £2,470 per customer, well above the £1,920 high-value threshold — and warrants 1:1 outreach. At Risk is the volume play: 164 customers and £370k of revenue, the most cost-effective bucket for a discount-led win-back. About to Sleep is the early-warning tier: a lighter-touch lifecycle nudge before they migrate into At Risk.

Three retention targets, three intensities  |  Customers, revenue, share of revenue, and a recommended action per segment.
  • Pool size Three target segments hold 341 customers (8%) and £574k of revenue (7.2%) — small enough to act on with focus, not mass spend.
  • VIP rescue first Cannot Lose Them: only 38 customers but £2,470 each on average — account-manager outreach and early-access offers, not bulk email.
  • Volume play second At Risk: 164 customers, £370,280 in revenue (4.7% of total) — the largest pool and the most cost-effective bucket for a discount-led win-back. About to Sleep gets a lighter lifecycle nudge.

Three campaigns at three intensities for 341 customers carrying £574k of revenue. The retention play sized correctly is a focused, near-term win — not a mass-marketing programme.

▶ Data Notes & Methodology (click to expand)

Source
UCI Online Retail dataset (~541k transactions from a UK gifts and homewares retailer, Dec 2010 – Dec 2011). All currency: GBP (£).
Cleaning policy (every rule logs a before/after row count)
  1. Trim period to 2010-12-01 → 2011-11-30 (12 full months) — the trailing 9 days of Dec 2011 corrupt cohort math. Drops 25,525 rows.
  2. Drop UnitPrice ≤ 0 — non-priced lines (manual adjustments) are not products. Drops 2,463 rows.
  3. Drop exact duplicates — 4,984 rows.
  4. Cancellations (InvoiceNo starting C or negative quantity): kept and netted against per-customer revenue rather than dropped wholesale. 8,892 rows kept.
  5. Drop missing CustomerID for customer-level analyses (RFM / LTV / cohorts) — ~25% of rows. Total revenue still uses these rows; segments and cohorts do not.
  6. Non-product StockCodes (POST, M, DOT, BANK CHARGES, etc.) audited (2,614 rows) but not removed — no product-level analysis is in scope.
  7. Drop net-negative customers (51 customers whose net Monetary went ≤ 0 after netting cancellations) — they would corrupt the LTV target.
RFM segmentation
Snapshot date = 2011-12-01. Recency, Frequency, and Monetary scored on independent quintile bins (1 = worst, 5 = best). The (R, F, M) score triple maps to one of 11 named segments via the standard Putler grid; the full mapping is exported as data/processed/rfm_segment_map.json for auditability.
Cohort retention
Cohort = year-month of first purchase. Period n = months elapsed. Retention[c, n] = share of cohort c with at least one invoice in period n. Cells outside the observed window are masked (grey) so the trend is not falsely depressed by the censor.
LTV-driver model
A gradient-boosted classifier (sklearn.ensemble.HistGradient­BoostingClassifier) predicting whether a customer falls in the top quintile of net Monetary (threshold £1,920). Reported with 5-fold stratified cross-validation: discrimination = 0.998, precision = 0.956, recall = 0.953. The discrimination figure is inflated by construction — the target is derived from M and one feature (Frequency) is structurally correlated with M. The body of the report intentionally focuses on the non-RFM features (basket size, unit price), which is the un-confounded signal.
Partial dependence on the top non-RFM features
Partial dependence — AvgBasketSize  |  Top non-RFM driver of top-quintile probability.
Partial dependence — AvgUnitPrice  |  Second non-RFM driver — flatter than basket size.
Caveats
  • Cancellations are netted, not dropped. Per-customer Monetary is a net figure (purchases − returns), matching how a CRM team would think about a customer's true contribution.
  • Missing CustomerID is kept for total revenue, dropped for customer-level work. Total revenue (£9.32M) is the upper bound; customer-attributed revenue (£7.94M) feeds segments and cohorts.
  • UK accounts for ~85% of customers and ~91% of rows. Non-UK is encoded as a binary feature in the LTV model rather than split into separate analyses.
  • Currency is GBP throughout. Treating the figures as USD would understate revenue by ~25% at 2011 rates.
